River Condition Prediction

Project Showcase by Team 7-A

View Prototypes

Problem Statement

How can we accurately predict river flow rate and conditions to enhance the safety of the public and protect wildlife, while also forecasting pollution levels downstream? Current data provided by SEPA is often difficult for the general public to interpret, especially for anticipating future conditions. There is also an increasing need to understand how the upstream flow rates impact downstream pollution. Our challenge lies in making this information more accessible, actionable, and predictive with possible integrations with weather data, AI modelling, and community-contributed observations.

Proposed Solution

Our solution focuses on simplifying river condition forecasting using a combination of existing SEPA data, weather integration, and user-contributed data. By applying AI models and intuitive visualisations, we aim to provide real-time and predictive insights into river safety and pollution trends. This will empower the public, local authorities, and environmental agencies with timely and clear information.

Justification

Our solution is justified by the urgent need for readily available, reliable, and predictive information on river condition, especially under climate change and risks of pollution. SEPA information currently available, though comprehensive, is not user-friendly to the public at large and lacks predictive quality. Our application of AI modelling in the treatment of weather information enhances the predictability of river behaviour patterns in the future, which is critical to both public safety and environmental planning.

By integrating community-contributed observations, we also integrate a citizen science component that improves not just data coverage, but also public engagement and awareness. The simple and intuitive visualisations employed ensure that complex data is translated into actionable insights, rendering the platform useful to a broad range of stakeholders—from residents to environmental agencies.

This multi-faceted solution addresses the root problems of inaccessibility and unpredictability in current systems and offers a solution that is technically feasible and socially useful.

Design Journey

Our design journey began with an in-depth discussion with our key stakeholder, Friends of the River Kelvin (FoRK). These meetings were crucial in helping us understand both the environmental challenges facing the river and the communication gaps between available data and the public. FoRK highlighted several pressing issues: a general lack of awareness and education around river health, the serious problem of sewage and chemical pollution in the UK, and the difficulty the public has in understanding the health impacts of poor water quality. Although groups such as the Kelvin Biodiversity Network had previously held exhibitions, their displays were mainly graphs and numbers — informative but lacking background stories or emotional connection.

Our discussions also revealed gaps in existing monitoring and public information. Unlike coasts and beaches, rivers in Glasgow do not display tide levels or safety conditions, and riverfly surveys only cover certain months, missing incidents outside of this period. Data from storms, chemical pollution, and other environmental factors is often not integrated or updated frequently enough. There was also a strong desire to encourage people to see the river as a place for leisure—canoeing, fishing, picnics—rather than simply a hazardous or “off-limits” space.

From these conversations, we identified several opportunities:

  • Using storytelling alongside data to make information more engaging.
  • Deploying posters with QR codes that link to live river condition updates, safety information, and community stories.
  • Incorporating gamification to motivate public participation in data-sharing (levelling systems, vouchers, virtual rewards).
  • Ensuring content is balanced—include both positive stories and necessary warnings to avoid discouraging engagement.
  • Exploring partnerships with groups like the Glasgow Humane Society for safety and educational outreach.

Inspired by FoRK’s feedback, we began prototyping solutions that could blend advocacy, education, and community participation. Our low-fidelity prototypes focused on layout concepts for physical posters, mobile-friendly dashboards, and community story pages. We incorporated FoRK’s suggestion of creating relatable mascots (e.g., penguins with umbrellas or sunglasses to reflect conditions) to make information fun and approachable. Mid-fidelity prototypes tested QR usability, simple data visualisations, and moderation workflows for user-submitted content.

Each iteration was shaped by further feedback—particularly around how to motivate ongoing contributions and how to ensure the platform remained trustworthy through curated, moderated content. By the high-fidelity stage, our designs combined AI-powered condition predictions, community-contributed observations, and story-based content, all accessible through both physical and digital touchpoints.

Looking back, starting with stakeholder conversations grounded our entire design process. These early meetings didn’t just highlight the data gaps — they helped us understand the human motivations, fears, and habits that ultimately shaped our final solution. This approach ensured our design was not only technically sound, but also socially relevant and community-driven.

What We're Proud Of

This project was a meaningful opportunity for our team to bridge the gap between complex environmental data and public understanding. At its core, our aim was to take raw, often inaccessible datasets — such as those from SEPA — and transform them into something that could directly inform and empower the community. The challenge was not only technical but also human: how do we make people care about river conditions and pollution trends in a way that inspires action? One of the strongest aspects of our journey was our iterative design process. We began with rough, low-fidelity prototypes to quickly explore ideas without being tied to a single vision. Each version was refined based on feedback from real users and stakeholders, enabling us to evolve from concept sketches into high-fidelity, functional designs. This cycle of testing and refinement helped ensure that our solution remained both informative and engaging, while also responsive to user needs. Collaboration played a central role. Working closely with stakeholders such as Friends of the River Kelvin gave us real-world insights into local concerns, technical constraints, and existing initiatives. Visiting the river ourselves added an observational layer — seeing the environment firsthand helped us better understand the challenges that data alone could not convey. This combination of stakeholder dialogue and on-site research grounded our ideas in reality. However, the journey was not without obstacles. We encountered limited data availability, which sometimes restricted our predictive modelling capabilities. Additionally, the technical complexity of the datasets meant that translating them into accurate, user-friendly forecasts required careful interpretation. A major ongoing challenge was finding the balance between educational content and visual appeal — too much information could overwhelm the user, but too little could undercut the platform’s purpose. From these challenges came our most important lesson: storytelling is vital for public engagement. We realised that raw data, no matter how accurate, is not enough to motivate people. It needs to be presented in a way that is relatable, visually clear, and emotionally resonant. By incorporating narrative elements, real-world examples, and intuitive visualisations, we created a platform that not only informs but also inspires curiosity and care for local rivers. We are proud of how far we progressed in a relatively short time. Our solution demonstrates how the fusion of technology, design, and human-centered thinking can make a real difference in environmental communication. More than just a technical achievement, it’s a step toward fostering stronger connections between people and their natural surroundings — encouraging the public to not just consume information, but to act on it for the benefit of both the community and the environment.

Team Reflection

As a team, we are grateful for the invaluable opportunity to meet with Sally from Friends of the River Kelvin (FoRK) and engage in meaningful discussions about the environmental challenges faced by the River Kelvin. This meeting was not only a key milestone in our project but also a learning experience that broadened our understanding of the wider environmental, social, and community contexts in which our work is situated.

One of the key themes we took away from our conversation with Sally is the complexity of river conservation efforts. It became clear that environmental issues such as pollution, biodiversity loss, and fluctuating water quality are deeply interconnected with community engagement, local behaviour, and public awareness. Through Sally’s insights, we recognised that technical solutions alone are not enough; the success of any intervention depends on fostering a sense of ownership and responsibility within the local community. This has influenced the way we now frame our project goals — shifting from a purely data-driven approach to one that integrates accessible communication and community outreach.

From multiple perspectives, we also learned that river health cannot be seen in isolation. Wider factors such as urban development, climate patterns, local regulations, and even volunteer capacity all play a role in shaping the state of the river. Sally’s perspective, grounded in years of hands-on experience with FoRK, helped us appreciate the value of grassroots action and the realities of implementing solutions on the ground. As a result, our project is now more attuned to the needs and priorities of those actively working to protect the river.

This reflection has led to genuine learning for our team. We have translated these lessons into actionable insights by revisiting our project design to ensure our outputs are not only scientifically sound but also publicly accessible and easy to interpret. For example, we are incorporating more user-friendly data visualisations and considering ways to present predictive information in formats that local residents can readily understand and act upon.

We would like to sincerely thank Sally for her generosity with her time, her openness in sharing the challenges FoRK faces, and her willingness to collaborate with us. Her contributions have helped shape our project into something more impactful, relevant, and grounded in the lived reality of those who care deeply for the River Kelvin. We look forward to continuing this collaboration and contributing meaningfully to the shared goal of safeguarding the river’s future.
